# Fundamental Analysis Reference

## Valuation Ratios

### P/E Ratio (Price to Earnings)
- Stock price ÷ Earnings per share (EPS)
- Tells you: how much you're paying for $1 of earnings
- **Interpretation depends heavily on sector and growth rate**
- Tech/growth: 25–50 can be normal. Value/mature: 10–20 is standard. >50 = priced for perfection
- **Forward P/E** (based on next year's expected earnings) more useful than trailing P/E
- Compare always to: sector average, historical average, direct competitors

### PEG Ratio (P/E to Growth)
- P/E ÷ Earnings growth rate
- **<1.0** = potentially undervalued relative to growth
- **1.0** = fairly valued
- **>2.0** = expensive relative to growth
- Most useful for growth stocks where P/E alone misleads

### P/B Ratio (Price to Book)
- Stock price ÷ Book value per share
- More relevant for banks, financials, asset-heavy companies
- **<1.0** = trading below book value (value signal or distress signal)
- **1–3** = reasonable for most companies
- **>5** = significant premium, justified only if ROE is high

### EV/EBITDA (Enterprise Value to EBITDA)
- Better than P/E for comparing companies with different debt levels
- **<10** = generally attractive
- **10–15** = fair
- **>20** = expensive (justified for high-growth companies)
- Sector median is the real benchmark

### P/S Ratio (Price to Sales)
- Used for unprofitable companies or early-stage growth
- **<1** = cheap
- **1–5** = typical range
- **>10** = very expensive, high growth expectations baked in

## Profitability Metrics

### Gross Margin
- (Revenue - Cost of Goods Sold) ÷ Revenue
- Shows pricing power and production efficiency
- **Software/SaaS**: 60–80%+ is typical
- **Retail**: 20–40%
- **Manufacturing**: 30–50%
- Trend matters: expanding margins = positive, contracting = red flag

### Operating Margin
- Operating Income ÷ Revenue
- After operating expenses, before interest and taxes
- Declining over multiple quarters = efficiency problem or pricing pressure

### Net Margin
- Net Income ÷ Revenue
- Compare to sector peers, not absolute thresholds

### ROE (Return on Equity)
- Net Income ÷ Shareholders' Equity
- **>15%** = good
- **>20%** = excellent
- High ROE + low debt = genuine quality. High ROE + high debt = leveraged quality (riskier)

### ROA (Return on Assets)
- Net Income ÷ Total Assets
- **>5%** = acceptable
- **>10%** = strong
- Better than ROE for capital-heavy industries (banks, industrials)

### ROIC (Return on Invested Capital)
- The gold standard of capital efficiency
- NOPAT ÷ Invested Capital
- **>10%** = good
- **>WACC** = creating value for shareholders
- Consistently high ROIC = competitive moat

## Growth Metrics

### Revenue Growth (YoY)
- Compare to sector growth rate, not just a number
- Decelerating growth is more important than absolute growth rate
- **Flag:** Revenue growing but earnings not = profitability problem

### EPS Growth
- Net income growing + share count stable = real EPS growth
- EPS growing but via buybacks, not earnings = financial engineering

### Free Cash Flow (FCF)
- Operating Cash Flow - Capital Expenditures
- The most honest profitability metric — harder to manipulate than EPS
- **FCF Yield** = FCF ÷ Market Cap. >5% = attractive
- Watch: earnings high but FCF low = quality concern

## Balance Sheet Health

### Debt-to-Equity Ratio
- Total Debt ÷ Shareholders' Equity
- **<0.5** = conservative
- **0.5–1.5** = moderate, manageable
- **>2** = high leverage, risk in rising rate environment
- Context: utilities and banks operate at higher leverage by nature

### Current Ratio
- Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities
- **>1.5** = healthy liquidity
- **<1** = potential short-term liquidity stress

### Interest Coverage Ratio
- EBIT ÷ Interest Expense
- **>5** = comfortable
- **<2** = dangerous, especially in high-rate environment

### Net Cash vs Net Debt
- Cash - Total Debt
- Net cash positive = fortress balance sheet (can weather downturns, fund growth internally)
- Heavy net debt = risk in recessions, rate hikes

## Sector-Specific Benchmarks

### Technology / Software (SaaS)
Key: Revenue growth rate, gross margin, Rule of 40 (Revenue growth % + EBITDA margin % > 40), Net Revenue Retention, ARR growth
Typical multiples: EV/Revenue 5–15x for growth, EV/EBITDA 20–40x

### Banking / Financials
Key: Net Interest Margin (NIM), NPAs (Non-Performing Assets), Capital Adequacy Ratio, ROE
Typical multiples: P/B 1–3x, P/E 8–15x

### Consumer / Retail
Key: Same-store sales growth, inventory turns, gross margin trends
Typical multiples: P/E 15–25x, EV/EBITDA 10–15x

### Healthcare / Pharma
Key: Pipeline (Phase 1/2/3 drugs), patent cliff dates, R&D % of revenue
Typical multiples: P/E wide range (10x for generics, 30x+ for innovative)

### Energy
Key: Oil/gas reserves, production costs, free cash flow at different commodity prices
Typical multiples: EV/EBITDA 5–10x

### Real Estate (REITs)
Key: Funds From Operations (FFO), occupancy rates, debt levels
Typical multiples: Price/FFO 15–20x

## Red Flags — When Fundamentals Look Bad

| Red Flag | What It Suggests |
|---|---|
| Revenue declining + management blaming "macro" | Structural problem, not cyclical |
| EPS growing but FCF declining | Accounting tricks, earnings quality poor |
| Insider selling in bulk (not just RSU vesting) | Insiders know something |
| Debt ballooning + rising rates | Balance sheet risk |
| Goodwill = large % of total assets | Overpaid for acquisitions, risk of impairment |
| Auditor changes or qualified audit opinion | Accounting concerns |
| Guidance consistently missed | Management credibility problem |
| Gross margins declining while revenue grows | Scaling isn't working, competition |

---

## Green Flags — What Great Fundamentals Look Like

- Revenue growth + margin expansion simultaneously
- Consistent FCF generation for 3+ years
- ROIC well above WACC
- Net cash position or rapidly declining debt
- Insider buying (especially CEO/board level)
- Raising guidance or beating/raising every quarter
- Market share gains in growing market
- Competitive moat visible in pricing power (margins stable despite inflation)
